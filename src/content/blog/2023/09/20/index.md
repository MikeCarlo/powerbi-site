AI is showing up everywhere in the analytics stack, but “use AI” isn’t a strategy. The real question is **where it reduces friction without adding risk**.

In Ep. 251, Mike, Tommy, and Seth talk through practical places to apply AI in BI—especially in the messy middle of projects (requirements, definitions, documentation, and iteration). The theme: use AI to speed up thinking and drafting, but keep humans accountable for truth, security, and business definitions.

## Main Discussion

A useful way to think about AI in BI is: **AI is great at drafts and options; terrible at accountability.** It can help you brainstorm measures, rephrase requirements, generate documentation scaffolding, and explain patterns—but you still need governed definitions, controlled data access, and validation.

Takeaways you can apply:

- **Use AI to clarify the question, not to invent the answer**: start with “What decision are we trying to make?” and “What would change our behavior?” before you generate any visuals.
- **Treat AI-generated DAX as a first draft**: ask for multiple approaches, then validate with test cases and known totals—especially around filter context.
- **Automate documentation and naming consistency**: AI is strong at turning existing model metadata into readable descriptions, measure explanations, and field lists that stakeholders can actually use.
- **Keep governance and security non-negotiable**: if the tool can see sensitive data, assume it will leak unless you’ve explicitly designed for isolation, auditing, and least privilege.
- **Use AI to improve communication**: have it rewrite technical findings into executive language, or turn a messy meeting note into a crisp set of requirements and acceptance criteria.
- **Build a “verification loop” into your workflow**: every AI suggestion should end in something checkable—numbers, rules, sources, or reproducible steps.
- **Don’t skip semantic modeling**: AI can speed up building, but it can’t rescue a model with unclear definitions, duplicated logic, and unowned metrics.

## Looking Forward

Pick one safe, high-leverage use case (documentation, requirements drafting, or DAX prototyping) and standardize it with guardrails—then expand only after you can prove it improves quality, not just speed.
