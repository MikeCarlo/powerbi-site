## Main Discussion

This episode is a holiday-flavored chat that quickly pivots into a deep dive on **Organizational Apps (Org Apps)**: what they are, why Microsoft introduced them, and how they differ from the classic **workspace app** experience in Power BI.

A key takeaway is that Org Apps feel like the “Fabric-native” evolution of the app concept — a container meant to package and distribute content, but in a way that aligns with Fabric’s broader set of artifacts.

### Workspace Apps vs. Org Apps

The group frames the comparison around a few practical differences:

- **Multiplicity:** workspace apps have historically been a one-workspace-to-one-app model (with audiences helping segment content), while Org Apps support creating **multiple apps** from the same workspace.
- **Artifact scope:** Org Apps expand the concept of “what can be distributed” beyond just the traditional Power BI objects, with a big focus on **notebooks** (including view-only experiences) and **real-time dashboards**.
- **User experience customization:** Org Apps bring more flexibility around navigation, branding, and layout choices — removing some of the friction points people hit with the older app UI customization options.

### Why Notebooks Inside an App Is a Big Deal

One of the most interesting parts of the conversation is the implication of sharing a **notebook** as part of an app experience.

Notebooks can be more than code: they can tell a story with markdown, images, queries, and visuals — and if they’re view-only, they give consumers a guided experience without turning everyone into an editor. That opens up a new “data product” style distribution pattern where you’re not only shipping a report, you’re shipping context and analysis alongside it.

### Security, Propagation, and the Preview Gotchas

The team also gets into the part that can make or break adoption: **permissions**.

Org Apps can change how access to related items (especially across workspaces) is granted, which is helpful when reports depend on semantic models living somewhere else. But they also call out an important preview limitation: in some cases, Org Apps can **propagate access** to items without automatically revoking it when users are removed or when items are removed from the Org App.

If you’re piloting Org Apps, treat access management and cleanup as part of the rollout plan — especially in organizations where access reviews and permission reporting are already challenging.

## Looking Forward

Org Apps are a clear signal that Microsoft is continuing to invest in Fabric-first experiences for content packaging and distribution. If your organization is moving deeper into Fabric and you’re already using apps as the primary way users discover and consume content, Org Apps are worth piloting — just be deliberate about governance, audience strategy, and the current preview limitations.
